## 2. Per-claim validation table

| # | Claim | Verdict | Citation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Schedule of Accounts" is the canonical broker→factor batch handoff term | **PARTIALLY VALIDATED.** The term is widely used in commercial-finance factoring (it appears in IFA member documentation and standard factoring agreements), but in *transportation* factoring vendors lean toward the term "**funding schedule**" / "**funding batch**" / "**invoice submission**" via portal or TMS integration. Format is NEVER a public CSV spec — it's factor-specific portal upload (web or TMS API) with PDF attachments. | Sources 1, 19 (eCapital Master Agreement uses "Purchased Account" / "Schedule" implicitly), 24, OTR/Vektor TMS integration write-up. **CHL should treat "Schedule of Accounts" as the legal term and "funding schedule" as the operational term — both refer to the same thing.** |
| 2a | Broker stamps NOA on its own invoice (vs factor providing pre-stamped template) | **VALIDATED both ways exist.** Triumph (source 14) explicitly states: *"NOAs can be presented by an invoice 'stamp', separate communication (letter) or both."* Apex (source 7) states the *factor* sends the NOA letter to the debtor at relationship start; the broker/carrier client typically also prints a remittance-redirect notice on each invoice. Industry-standard is BOTH: factor-issued one-time NOA letter + perpetual remittance stamp on every invoice. | Sources 7, 14, 26, 30. |
| 2b | UCC Article 9 requirements for enforceable NOA | **VALIDATED + REFINED.** UCC § 9-406 requires the notification be: (a) **authenticated** by assignor OR assignee; (b) **reasonably identify the rights assigned**; (c) inform the account debtor that the amount due/to-become-due has been assigned; (d) direct payment to the assignee. Account debtor may request **reasonable proof** of assignment "seasonably"; if assignee fails to provide, debtor may keep paying the original party. | Sources 3, 5, 6 (Cornell LII verbatim). |
| 3 | Advance rate range typical 80-95% (CHL operator) / 85-95% (v3) | **VALIDATED — both ranges correct depending on programme.** FreightWaves Checkpoint (source 25): industry typical 70–95%. Apex (source 8): 80–95% on freight. Triumph (sources 14, 15): 85–95%, marketing claim up to 100%. OTR (source 16): **96% non-recourse / 92% recourse**, BOLT plans up to 100%. TBS (source 18): up to 95%, full 100% on non-recourse with no holdback. | Sources 8, 14, 16, 18, 25. **CHL's 85-95% v3 is the safe central band.** |
| 4 | Reserve range typical 5-20% holdback released after broker pays | **VALIDATED — central band 5-10%, full range 0-15%.** TBS (source 18) explicitly notes: *"most factors hold 5-10% until the broker pays."* OTR true-non-recourse: **no reserve holdback**. Apex (source 8): reserve = invoice value − advance − fee, released on broker payment. eCapital Master Agreement (source 19) defines Reserve Account as *"a bookkeeping account on the books of the Purchaser representing an unpaid portion of the Purchase Price."* | Sources 8, 16, 18, 19. **CHL's 5-20% range is wider than industry — tighten to 5-15% with note that non-recourse plans may carry 0%.** |
| 5 | Factor fee 1.5-5% per industry, flat or % | **VALIDATED.** Apex (source 8): 1–5% advertised, 2.5–3.5% common for small fleets, both flat and tiered. Triumph (source 14): 1.5–3.5% in 2026, non-recourse starts at 2%. OTR (source 16): 2.5–5%. TBS (source 18): 1.25–5%, can spike to 7.5% on tiered if broker takes 6 weeks. | Sources 8, 14, 16, 18. **CHL's 1.5-5% range is correct industry consensus.** |
| 6 | Recourse vs non-recourse — dominant transportation-broker convention | **MIXED — both common; trend toward non-recourse marketing.** Recourse is operationally dominant (most factors default to recourse with non-recourse as upsell at higher fee). FreightWaves Checkpoint (source 23): recourse cheaper, non-recourse priced 0.5-1.5pt higher. OTR markets "true non-recourse" as differentiator (source 16). Chargeback occurs when broker fails to pay within recourse period. | Sources 16, 17, 23. |
| 7 | Verification call to shipper to verify delivery — universal? | **VALIDATED — universal but channel varies.** Bankers Factoring (source 27): factors verify *"the load was delivered, the customer is creditworthy, and paperwork is complete."* Capstone (source 28): three channels — phone call to AP, email to AP, online portal lookup. eCapital (source 21): fraud-flag verification is mandatory pre-funding. Timeline 1–3 business days, same-day on portal-lookup-eligible debtors. | Sources 21, 27, 28. **CHL should expect verification on every invoice, not sampling.** |
| 8 | Most common factor decline reasons | **VALIDATED — five primary decline buckets.** (a) Shipper/broker credit insufficient (source 33), (b) **Customer concentration >20-30%** of book (source 30: Integrity Factoring explicit), (c) Fraud signals — carrier dark on track-and-trace, identity-theft red flags (sources 2, 31), (d) Aging — invoice already >recourse age, (e) Lane/debtor blacklist — broker on factor's known-slow-pay list (Debtor411 maintained, source 32). | Sources 2, 30, 31, 32, 33. |
| 9 | Clean-packet 4-doc list (broker invoice + signed Rate Con + signed POD + accessorial receipts) | **VALIDATED + ONE ADDITION.** Industry standard list per multiple factors: (1) broker invoice, (2) signed rate confirmation / rate-con, (3) **signed Bill of Lading (BOL)** AND/OR signed Proof of Delivery (POD), (4) accessorial / lumper receipts. Apex freight-bill template (source 9) confirms. **Note: BOL and POD are sometimes treated separately — POD is the BOL with signature/timestamp showing delivery completion. Full clean packet is BOL + POD if separate documents.** | Sources 9, plus general industry consensus across sources 24, 27. **CHL v3's 4-doc list is correct; recommend treating BOL/POD as paired-or-equivalent rather than single doc.** |
| 10 | Shipper credit pre-screening via portal lookup (D&B / Cortera / Equifax Commercial) | **VALIDATED — multi-bureau + transportation-specific.** Standard sources: Dun & Bradstreet, Experian Commercial, Equifax Commercial, Cortera (now part of Moody's Analytics), Ansonia (source 33). Transportation-specific: **Debtor411** (source 32) updates weekly from factor-contributed AR data — the single biggest factor-industry private DB. Most factors run shipper credit pre-booking AND continuously, not just at invoice submission. Carriers/brokers can typically request free credit pulls through their factor's portal. | Sources 32, 33. |
| 11 | Carrier-side NOA vs Broker-side NOA semantics | **VALIDATED — distinct contexts, identical legal mechanism.** Carrier-side: factor's client = carrier; debtor = broker (or shipper-direct on direct-shipper carrier loads); NOA tells broker "pay our factor not us." Broker-side: factor's client = broker; debtor = shipper; NOA tells shipper "pay our factor not the broker." Both governed by UCC § 9-406. **For CHL (broker-with-factoring), the NOA flow is broker-side: shipper receives NOA stamp on broker invoice + factor letter. The carrier-side NOA flowing INBOUND from the carrier's factor is a SEPARATE artifact CHL must process when paying carriers.** | Sources 3, 26, 14, 7. |
| 12 | First-time invoice vs ongoing — higher first-time fee? | **NOT VALIDATED as a rule.** No primary source describes a "first-invoice premium." Origination/setup fees DO exist (Triumph: $300 origination, source 14), but per-invoice fee is contractually constant. Some factors charge ACH fee per funding ($5-10) which is volume-uniform. **Operator's prior assumption of first-time premium is unsupported by industry sources.** | Sources 8, 14 (no premium reported). |
| 13 | Invoice aging policy — write off / chargeback at 60 or 90 days? | **VALIDATED — 90 days is industry default.** Recourse period typically 60–90 days. After that, factor charges back against reserve or future advances. Some factors (Summar referenced in source on chargebacks) market aging-coverage as differentiator. Non-recourse generally covers debtor *bankruptcy/insolvency* but NOT slow-pay aging — common operator misconception. | Sources 23, plus general industry consensus. |

## 4. Discoveries CHL missed (NEW findings)

1. **Debtor411 is the transportation-factor private credit DB** (source 32). Factors contribute receivable-aging data weekly and pull broker/shipper credit reports back. CHL should know this is the system that decides whether their *factor* will fund their invoice on a given shipper. CHL should also recognise that **as a broker with a factor, CHL's own pay-to-carrier behaviour will end up in Debtor411** — slow-pay carriers will report through their own factors. This is a reputation surface CHL must protect.

2. **FMCSA October-2025 MC→USDOT consolidation** (source 35). FMCSA phased out separate MC numbers for brokers/carriers, transitioning to single USDOT identifier, AND raised broker surety bond from $10K → $75K. Plus enhanced broker applicant vetting. CHL should ensure all factor-side and carrier-vetting code uses USDOT as the canonical key, with MC as legacy alias.

3. **eCapital's broker factoring master agreement is publicly readable** (source 19) and contains formal definitions for Advance Percentage, Purchased Account, Reserve Account, and Section 7.7(d) power-of-attorney for NOA preparation. **This is the closest thing to a Tier-1 sample broker-factoring legal document publicly accessible.** CHL should bookmark this as the structural template for what its own factor relationship will look like.

4. **NOA "authenticated by assignor OR assignee"** (UCC § 9-406, source 3) — this is critical. CHL's broker invoice can carry the NOA stamp authenticated by CHL itself (the assignor) and that meets the statutory requirement, OR the factor can send its own letter authenticated as the assignee. Both have legal force. The "stamp is enough" rule means broker-controlled invoice templates are sufficient — CHL doesn't have to wait for factor-issued letter copies on a per-invoice basis.

5. **"Seasonable proof of assignment" requirement** (UCC § 9-406, source 3) — if a shipper requests proof, the factor MUST provide reasonable proof seasonably or the shipper can keep paying CHL. This means CHL's factor relationship should pre-stage proof-of-assignment documents (UCC-1 financing statement copies, executed factoring agreement excerpts) for fast disclosure to skeptical shippers.

6. **Customer concentration limits 20-30%** (source 30, Integrity Factoring) — CHL should track per-shipper concentration in its receivables ledger and proactively diversify before the factor flags it. **Concentration is one of the top 5 decline reasons.**

7. **Carrier-side inbound NOA processing** is a separate workflow CHL needs as a broker. When CHL pays a carrier, the carrier's factor sends CHL an NOA. CHL must (a) record that NOA, (b) update its carrier-payment routing to the factor's remit address, (c) honour the NOA on every subsequent invoice from that carrier or face double-pay liability (source 7 explicit).
