### § 371.2 — Definitions

**Verbatim text** (S3, S12):

> "**Broker** means a person who, for compensation, arranges, or offers to arrange, the transportation of property by an authorized motor carrier. Motor carriers, or persons who are employees or bona fide agents of carriers, are not brokers within the meaning of this section when they arrange or offer to arrange the transportation of shipments which they are authorized to transport and which they have accepted and legally bound themselves to transport."

> "**Bona fide agents** are persons who are part of the normal organization of a motor carrier and perform duties under the carrier's directions pursuant to a preexisting agreement which provides for a continuing relationship, precluding the exercise of discretion on the part of the agent in allocating traffic between the carrier and others."

> "**Brokerage or brokerage service** is the arranging of transportation or the physical movement of a motor vehicle or of property. It can be performed on behalf of a motor carrier, consignor, or consignee."

> "**Non-brokerage service** is all other service performed by a broker on behalf of a motor carrier, consignor, or consignee."

**Amendment history:** [45 FR 68942, Oct. 17, 1980; redesignated at 61 FR 54707, Oct. 21, 1996; amended at 89 FR 90618, Nov. 18, 2024]

> ⚠️ **Recent amendment.** The Nov 18 2024 amendment (89 FR 90618) is final-rule "Definitions of Broker and Bona Fide Agents" — the FMCSA codification of long-standing interim guidance. The verbatim text above reflects the post-amendment definitions.

**Plain-English:** Defines who is/is not a "broker" for federal regulatory purposes. The definitional line between "broker" and "carrier with bona fide agents" matters because bona fide agents are NOT brokers and therefore are not subject to Part 371. CHL holds broker authority outright, so the question is moot for our own operations — but it matters for **carrier vetting** (chameleon agents posing as carriers).

**CHL state:** ✅ **COMPLIANT.**

**Artifact reference:**
- `C:\CHL\backend\broker_carrier_agreement.py` lines 49-55 — BCA template hard-codes "Continental Haul Logistics LLC", MC-1817555, broker-status framing. Aligns with the "broker" definition.
- `C:\CHL\frontend\src\views\CompliancePages.jsx` lines 140-146 — public terms-of-service page: *"CHL is a freight brokerage licensed by the U.S. Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (USDOT 4569843 · MC-1817555) and bonded with a $75,000 BMC-84 surety bond ... We arrange the transportation of property by contracted motor carriers; we do not operate trucks."* This is verbatim alignment with the § 371.2 broker definition.

### § 371.3 — Records to be Kept by Brokers ⭐ (Core compliance section)

**Verbatim text** (S9, S10):

> "**(a)** A broker shall keep a record of each transaction. For purposes of this section, brokers may keep master lists of consignors and the address and registration number of the carrier, rather than repeating this information for each transaction. The record shall show:
>
> **(1)** The name and address of the consignor;
> **(2)** The name, address, and registration number of the originating motor carrier;
> **(3)** The bill of lading or freight bill number;
> **(4)** The amount of compensation received by the broker for the brokerage service performed and the name of the payer;
> **(5)** A description of any non-brokerage service performed in connection with each shipment or other activity, the amount of compensation received for the service, and the name of the payer; and
> **(6)** The amount of any freight charges collected by the broker and the date of payment to the carrier.
>
> **(b)** Brokers shall keep the records required by this section for a period of three years.
>
> **(c)** Each party to a brokered transaction has the right to review the record of the transaction required to be kept by these rules."

**Amendment history:** [45 FR 68942, Oct. 17, 1980; redesignated at 61 FR 54707, Oct. 21, 1996, as amended at 62 FR 15421, Apr. 1, 1997]

**Plain-English:** Every brokered shipment must produce a six-element record. Records must be retained for at least 3 years. Any party (shipper / carrier / consignor / consignee) may request and review the record for a transaction they were party to. NPRM 89 FR 91648 proposes to upgrade this to (i) electronic-format records, (ii) 48-hour response to party request, (iii) ban on contractual waivers — but as of 2026-05-09, **only the current text above is binding**. (See § 7 below for NPRM status.)

### § 371.9 — Rebating and Compensation

**Verbatim text** (S6):

> "**(a)** A broker shall not charge or receive compensation from a motor carrier for brokerage service where:
>
> **(1)** The broker owns or has a material beneficial interest in the shipment, or
> **(2)** The broker is able to exercise control over the shipment because the broker owns the shipper, the shipper owns the broker, or there is common ownership of the two.
>
> **(b)** A broker shall not give or offer to give anything of value to any shipper, consignor or consignee (or their officers or employees) except inexpensive advertising items given for promotional purposes."

**Amendment history (presumed):** [45 FR 68942, Oct. 17, 1980; redesignated at 61 FR 54707, Oct. 21, 1996] — Cornell page did not surface a specific footer; standard Part 371 redesignation applies.

**Plain-English:** Two-prong anti-rebating rule. Prong (a): a broker cannot collect a brokerage fee from a carrier on a shipment where the broker has a material beneficial interest in the cargo, or where there's common ownership between the broker entity and the shipper entity (anti-self-dealing). Prong (b): brokers cannot give shippers/consignors/consignees anything of value (cash, gifts, kickbacks) beyond inexpensive promotional items (pens, calendars, etc.) — anti-kickback rule.

**CHL state:** 🟡 **PARTIAL — policy gap, no operational risk today.**

**Artifact reference:**
- **No dedicated § 371.9 enforcement code exists.** Grep of `C:\CHL\backend` for "rebat", "kickback", "material beneficial interest", "inexpensive advertising" returns zero hits.
- **Inferred operational compliance:** CHL is a single-entity brokerage with no documented common ownership of any shipper or carrier, and no documented gift/promo program targeting shipper officers. Today, by virtue of CHL's structure, prong (a) cannot be violated and prong (b) is not active.
- **Gap categories:**
  - 🟡 **No COI (conflict-of-interest) disclosure on shipper onboarding.** A shipper-broker agreement template (per EEE1 backlog) should include a COI representation: "Shipper represents and warrants that no officer/director/owner of Shipper has a material beneficial ownership interest in Broker, and vice versa."
  - 🟡 **No internal gift/promo policy doc.** CHL should publish (internal) a Promotional-Items Policy: "Inexpensive promo items (≤$25 per item, ≤$50 per recipient per year, must bear CHL branding) are permitted under § 371.9(b). Cash gifts, gift cards, dinners >$50, sports tickets, and similar items of value are PROHIBITED."
  - 🟡 **No carrier compensation gating on common-ownership detection.** The `db.shippers` and `db.carriers` collections do not record EIN cross-checks against CHL's officer/owner roster. A monthly cron that flags any shipper or carrier whose recorded EIN matches an EIN in `db.officers` or `db.related_parties` would close the loop.

**Effort to remediate:**
- Promo policy doc: 1 hour (document only, no code).
- COI representation in shipper-broker agreement template: 30 minutes (template extension).
- EIN cross-check cron: 2-4 hours (low priority — single-entity solo broker has near-zero blast radius today; revisit if CHL acquires another LLC or hires sales reps with carrier/shipper ownership stakes).

### § 371.13 — Accounting

**Verbatim text** (S7):

> "Each broker who engages in any other business shall maintain accounts so that the revenues and expenses relating to the brokerage portion of its business are segregated from its other activities. Expenses that are common shall be allocated on an equitable basis; however, the broker must be prepared to explain the basis for the allocation."

**Amendment history:** [45 FR 68943, Oct. 17, 1980]

**Plain-English:** If a broker also does anything else (e.g., owns trucks under a separate carrier authority, runs a warehouse, sells software), the brokerage P&L must be ledger-segregated from non-brokerage activities. Common overhead can be split, but the broker must be able to defend the allocation methodology if challenged (FMCSA audit, IRS audit, civil discovery).

> 🚨 **Mission-brief correction.** The mission brief stated "broker's settlement period (15 days)" under § 371.13. **There is NO 15-day settlement-period rule in § 371.13 or anywhere else in 49 CFR Part 371.** The 15-day reference may be confused with one of (a) the 7-day BMC-84 bond replenishment SLA (49 CFR § 387.307, MAP-21 financial responsibility rule effective Jan 16 2026), (b) common Net-30 / Net-15 commercial settlement terms in broker-carrier agreements, or (c) a state-level prompt-pay statute. **§ 371.13 only addresses ledger segregation when the broker engages in any other business — it does not impose any settlement-period requirement.**

**CHL state:** ✅ **COMPLIANT (definitionally, as a pure-broker single-entity).**

**Artifact reference:**
- CHL is **not currently** "engaged in any other business" of a kind that would require ledger segregation — CHL holds broker authority only, no carrier authority, no warehouse, no separately-traded software product. The § 371.13 obligation is triggered when CHL adds a second business line.
- `C:\CHL\backend\company_identity.py` records single legal entity Continental Haul Logistics LLC, MC-1817555 broker authority only.
- `C:\CHL\backend\finance_strategy.py` references Mercury Bank as the operating account; one bank account, one entity, one chart of accounts implicit.

**Gap:** **None today, but conditional gap for future growth.** When CHL adds a second business line (most likely path: ASSET broker — operating own trucks under a carrier MC alongside the broker MC), § 371.13 becomes a hard requirement. Plan to ship at that time:
- Separate QuickBooks / accounting-software classes for "Brokerage" vs "Carrier" vs "Other."
- Documented common-expense allocation methodology (typical: revenue-pro-rata or headcount-pro-rata).
- Annual review of allocation methodology by external CPA.

**Operator action:** Verify with CPA / tax preparer at year-end that current single-entity bookkeeping satisfies § 371.13 in light of any non-brokerage activities (e.g., investor capital raises, software licensing, training/consulting income). If any non-brokerage activity exists, reclassify accounts now rather than retroactively.
